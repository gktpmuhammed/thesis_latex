\chapter{Experiments and Results}\label{chapter:experiments}

\section{Experimental Objectives and Research Questions}\label{sec:exp_objectives}
This chapter evaluates whether organ-aware token design and decoder choice improve 3D CT report generation quality under a consistent CT-RATE protocol. The experiments are structured around four research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Token Interface):} Does organ-token interface design (multi-token baseline vs compressed/sentinel/positional variants) improve global clinical-report metrics?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Architecture):} Do heavier visual modules (Perceiver resampler, multiscale fusion, hybrid CNN+ViT) outperform the simpler masked cross-attention baseline?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Training Strategy):} How much do curriculum learning, hard-example mining, and imbalance-aware weighting change report quality?
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Decoder Family):} Under the same organ-attention visual frontend, how do MedGemma, GPT-2, and BioBART compare?
\end{itemize}

The chapter first reports completed MedGemma ablations, then presents the decoder-comparison structure and current run-status diagnostics for GPT-2/BioBART.

\subsection{Preprocessing Protocol}
We follow the unified preprocessing pipeline described in Chapter~\ref{chapter:methodology}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item HU clipping and normalization,
    \item spatial harmonization to $(112,256,352)$,
    \item organ-mask extraction and stacking,
    \item LLM-based report decomposition into organ sections.
\end{enumerate}

This ensures that architecture and decoder comparisons are not confounded by preprocessing differences.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}\label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
We report complementary metrics grouped by linguistic overlap, semantic similarity, and clinical fidelity. This multi-view evaluation is necessary because lexical overlap alone can be misleading in radiology text generation.

\subsection{Surface-level (Natural Language Generation) Metrics}
\subsubsection{BLEU}
BLEU~\cite{papineni2002bleu} is an n-gram precision-based score. For candidate report $c$ and references $\{r\}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{BLEU} = \mathrm{BP}\cdot \exp\!\left(\sum_{n=1}^{N} w_n \log p_n\right),
\end{equation}
where $p_n$ is modified n-gram precision, $w_n$ are n-gram weights, and BP is the brevity penalty:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{BP}=\begin{cases}
        1 & c_{len} > r_{len}^* \\
        \exp\left(1-\frac{r_{len}^*}{c_{len}}\right) & \text{otherwise.}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
We report BLEU-4.

\subsubsection{ROUGE-L}
ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge} uses longest common subsequence (LCS):
\[
R_{LCS}=\frac{\mathrm{LCS}(c,r)}{|r|},\qquad
P_{LCS}=\frac{\mathrm{LCS}(c,r)}{|c|},
\]
\begin{equation}
F_{LCS}=\frac{(1+\beta^2)R_{LCS}P_{LCS}}{R_{LCS}+\beta^2P_{LCS}}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{METEOR}
METEOR~\cite{banerjee2005meteor} computes a recall-oriented harmonic mean with a fragmentation penalty:
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{mean}}=\frac{(1+\alpha)PR}{R+\alpha P},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{METEOR}=F_{\mathrm{mean}}\cdot (1-\mathrm{Penalty}).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{CIDEr}
CIDEr~\cite{vedantam2015cider} compares TF-IDF-weighted n-gram vectors:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CIDEr}_n(c,r)=\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\frac{\mathbf{g}^n(c)\cdot\mathbf{g}^n(r_i)}{\|\mathbf{g}^n(c)\|\|\mathbf{g}^n(r_i)\|}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Semantic Metric}
\subsubsection{BERTScore}
BERTScore~\cite{zhang2019bertscore} measures contextual embedding similarity between candidate and reference tokens and aggregates precision/recall/F1-style semantic overlap.

\subsection{Clinical and Structured Metrics}
\subsubsection{CheXbert}
CheXbert~\cite{smit2020chexbert} evaluates alignment on clinically meaningful observation labels (reported as micro-F1 summary in this chapter).

\subsubsection{RadGraph}
RadGraph~\cite{jain2021radgraph} evaluates overlap of extracted radiology entities and relations between generated and reference reports.

\subsubsection{GREEN}
GREEN~\cite{ostmeier2024green} targets factual consistency and clinically meaningful error sensitivity in generated radiology text.

\subsubsection{RadCliQ}
RadCliQ~\cite{yu2023radcliq} is a composite clinical metric designed to correlate better with human radiology judgment than pure lexical overlap.

\subsubsection{SRR-BERT}
SRR-BERT-style structured-report scoring is used as an additional clinically informed signal in our evaluation exports.

\subsection{Metric Interpretation Policy}
In this thesis, metric interpretation is anchored on GREEN, RadGraph, and report-level lexical-semantic metrics together. CheXbert is still reported, but interpreted cautiously for organ-decomposed CT text because it was designed around full-report label extraction workflows.

\section{Automatic Evaluation Pipeline and Artifact Standardization}\label{sec:exp_eval_pipeline}
\subsection{Pipeline}
For each model checkpoint:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Generate organ-wise reports and save \texttt{generated\_reports.csv} (or \texttt{generated\_reports\_gemma.csv}).
    \item Compute metrics with unified \texttt{radeval\_metrics.py}.
    \item Export standardized \texttt{metrics\_final.csv} containing GLOBAL and per-organ rows.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Metric Key Mapping}
The evaluation script maps RadEval keys into thesis column names (e.g., \texttt{green} $\to$ GREEN, \texttt{radgraph\_simple} $\to$ RadGraph, \texttt{radcliq-v1} $\to$ RadCliQ). This removes ambiguity when combining results from different variants.

\subsection{Interpretation by Ablation Axis}
\textbf{Token/bridge design:}
The positional + sentinel visual-prefix variant gives the strongest global gains across GREEN, BLEU-4, METEOR, ROUGE-L, CIDEr, BERTScore, RadGraph, and RadCliQ. Relative to the baseline, gains include +7.7\% GREEN, +14.2\% BLEU-4, +11.1\% ROUGE-L, and +10.3\% RadGraph.

\textbf{Encoder-side complexity:}
Perceiver and multiscale variants do not consistently beat the simpler baseline under the current training budget. Multiscale slightly improves GREEN but loses in most lexical/graph metrics. Perceiver underperforms broadly, suggesting optimization cost may outweigh representational benefit in this setup.

\textbf{Training strategy interventions:}
Hard-example mining and default-text undersampling are competitive but below baseline on core global metrics. Curriculum learning drops substantially across almost all metrics in this two-phase configuration.

\textbf{Metric disagreement:}
The best global model has very low CheXbert despite improvements in GREEN/RadGraph and lexical-semantic metrics. This confirms that single-metric conclusions are risky for decomposed organ-wise CT text. In this thesis we prioritize GREEN + RadGraph + lexical-semantic agreement for model ranking.

\subsection{How to Read the Attention Maps}
For each case, we recommend reading the overlays with the following checklist:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Does the queried organ attention coincide with the organ mask footprint?
    \item Are high-intensity regions anatomically contiguous or scattered?
    \item Is attention stable across axial/coronal/sagittal views?
    \item Are false activations concentrated in clinically confounding neighboring structures?
\end{enumerate}

This qualitative protocol will be used together with the quantitative ablations in Section~\ref{sec:exp_medgemma_results} to build a coherent explanation of model behavior.

\section{Threats to Validity and Interpretation Limits}\label{sec:exp_threats}
\textbf{Dataset anatomy mismatch:}
Chest CT does not uniformly cover all abdominal organs, creating support imbalance for organ-level generation and evaluation.

\textbf{Metric-domain mismatch:}
Some metrics (especially CheXbert) were developed for full-report chest labeling and may underrepresent quality in organ-decomposed, multi-organ CT narratives.

\textbf{Protocol comparability:}
External baselines such as fVLM are informative but not directly protocol-equivalent.

\textbf{Decoder status timing:}
As of March 2, 2026, MedGemma metrics are complete in the standardized pipeline, while GPT-2/BioBART v3 quantitative rows are pending final standardized metric generation from official output folders.

\textbf{Human evaluation:}
This chapter reports automated metrics only. Human evaluation and formal significance testing are not included in the current version and are therefore not used for claims.

\section{Chapter Summary}\label{sec:exp_summary}
The main empirical finding is consistent: under the current CT-RATE setup, MedGemma with positional visual prefixing is the strongest internal model across nearly all primary global metrics. Heavier encoder variants and training-schedule interventions did not surpass this setting in the current compute budget.

Decoder-family comparison is established as a core axis with fixed data and metric interfaces; MedGemma values are finalized and GPT-2/BioBART rows are ready to be populated from the official v3 runs once their standardized metric pass is complete.
