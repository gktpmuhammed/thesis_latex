\chapter{Experiments and Results}\label{chapter:experiments}

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp_setup}
\subsection{Objectives and Research Questions}
This chapter evaluates organ-aware 3D CT report generation under a consistent CT-RATE protocol. Results are organized by four research questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Token Interface):} Does organ-token interface design improve report quality?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Architecture):} Do heavier visual modules outperform the simpler masked cross-attention baseline?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Training Strategy):} How much do curriculum, hard-example, and imbalance policies change outcomes?
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Decoder Family):} Under the same frontend, how do MedGemma, BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART compare?
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Protocol}\label{sec:exp_eval_protocol}
\subsection{Metric Families}\label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
We report complementary metric groups. Lexical overlap is measured with BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}, ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, METEOR~\cite{banerjee2005meteor}, and CIDEr~\cite{vedantam2015cider}. Semantic similarity is measured with BERTScore~\cite{zhang2019bertscore}. Clinical and factual consistency is measured with CheXbert~\cite{smit2020chexbert}, RadGraph~\cite{jain2021radgraph}, GREEN~\cite{ostmeier2024green}, RadCliQ~\cite{yu2023radcliq}, and an SRR-BERT style score.
Here, CheXbert is the report-labeling evaluator and should not be conflated with the CheXpert chest X-ray dataset~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} used in model pretraining pipelines.

Formal equations are provided in Appendix~\ref{appendix:metric_equations}. Chapter-level interpretation focuses on metric behavior and agreement patterns.

\subsection{Metric Interpretation Policy}
Model ranking is anchored on GREEN and RadGraph, then cross-checked with lexical-semantic metrics. CheXbert is reported but interpreted cautiously for organ-decomposed CT text because its design assumptions are closer to full-report label extraction workflows.
Unless noted otherwise, internal metric values in this thesis are reported on a $0$--$100$ scale.

For decoder-family analysis, we also report generation-behavior diagnostics to detect template collapse. These include exact-report uniqueness ratio, top-$k$ template coverage, and effective-template ratio $\exp(H)/N$, where $H$ is Shannon entropy over normalized report-frequency counts and $N$ is the number of generated reports.

\subsection{Evaluation Reproducibility and Result Provenance}
All checkpoints are evaluated with one standardized workflow: report generation, metric computation with the same RadEval framework~\cite{xu-etal-2025-radeval}, and structured aggregation of global and organ-level scores. This removes evaluation variability and keeps model comparisons methodologically consistent.

Metric definitions and table semantics are fixed before comparison. As a result, every experiment in this chapter is interpreted under the same scoring rules.

Quantitative claims are drawn only from official runs produced under this shared protocol. MedGemma ablations, text-policy comparisons, and decoder-family comparisons all follow the same data split, preprocessing assumptions, and evaluation pipeline.

\subsection{Qualitative Protocol for Attention Maps}
Cross-attention maps are interpreted with the following checks: spatial overlap with the queried organ mask, compactness vs diffusion, stability across planes, and false activation in anatomically confounding neighbors.

\subsection{RQ1: Token Interface Design}
RQ1 is answered positively. Prefix-Stable-8T is the strongest global model and improves both the primary clinical metrics and the lexical-semantic metrics relative to Baseline-8T. The strongest gains are concentrated in overall report quality and clinical structure signals, which supports the effect of interface stabilization.

The same conclusion is supported by the mask-downsampling transition. MaxPool-8T remains close to Baseline-8T and does not produce comparable gains, so the main improvement is not explained by mask downsampling alone. The stronger effect comes from the stabilized visual-prefix design in Prefix-Stable-8T.

\subsection{RQ2: Architecture Complexity}
Table~\ref{tab:delta_vs_baseline} summarizes deltas against baseline for representative models. In the maxpool-derived architecture branch, heavier modules such as Perceiver, multiscale, and v3-alignment variants do not consistently outperform MaxPool-8T under the current training budget.

Within this branch, Multiscale-FPN is the strongest of the heavier variants, but its gains are modest and not uniform across metric families. Perceiver-Bridge and CNN-Align-v3 remain below MaxPool-8T on the main global signals, which weakens the claim that added architectural complexity is the main driver of quality in this setup.

The practical interpretation is that complexity helps only in narrow cases under the current budget and does not establish a robust global advantage. Under one-epoch training with the current optimization constraints, interface quality remains more reliable than heavier visual modules.

\subsection{RQ3: Training Strategy}
Training-policy interventions split into two branches. In the area-derived branch, Undersample-DefaultText and Weighted-Organ-Loss do not surpass Prefix-Stable-8T. In the maxpool-derived branch, Hard-Mining and Curriculum-2Phase do not surpass MaxPool-8T, and curriculum learning degrades most global metrics in the current two-phase configuration.

In the area-derived branch, the gap to Prefix-Stable-8T is clear across clinical and lexical-semantic groups. Undersample-DefaultText trails the reference model, and Weighted-Organ-Loss trails further. These results suggest that imbalance-control policies alone do not recover the gain provided by a stronger interface.

In the maxpool-derived branch, Hard-Mining stays closer to MaxPool-8T but still remains below it on the primary quality signals. Curriculum-2Phase shows the largest overall regression in this branch. This likely reflects instability or underfitting under the current schedule rather than a generally negative verdict on curriculum ideas.

\subsection{RQ4: Decoder Family Comparison}
Decoder comparison is a core axis with fixed protocol and table schema. Table~\ref{tab:decoder_comparison} now includes finalized MedGemma, fVLM-aligned BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART rows from the same standardized metric pass.

The current evidence shows a mixed but interpretable pattern. BERT-base provides the strongest overall GREEN and broad lexical-semantic quality signals under the shared protocol. MedGemma remains stronger on the relational-clinical structure axis represented by RadGraph. GPT-2 remains stronger than BioBART on most global dimensions, while BioBART is the weakest overall among the four completed decoders.

Because decoder prompting policies differ across families, organ-header completion is not used for cross-decoder ranking. Table~\ref{tab:decoder_sanity} reports only neutral run diagnostics such as sample count and average output length.

Table~\ref{tab:decoder_diversity} adds diversity diagnostics over normalized full reports. BERT-base shows the strongest repetition profile among the four decoders: only $41.3\%$ unique reports, $19.0\%$ coverage by the top five templates, and $48.2\%$ coverage by the top fifty templates. MedGemma shows much lower template reuse, with $82.0\%$ unique reports and $16.6\%$ top-fifty coverage.

\section{Chapter Summary and Minimal Limitations Note}\label{sec:exp_summary}
The strongest internal model under current protocol is Prefix-Stable-8T. The result pattern is consistent across RQ1 to RQ3: interface stabilization is more beneficial than heavier architectural modules or training-policy complexity at the current compute budget.

The decoder axis for RQ4 now has complete standardized rows for all targeted decoder families in this thesis: MedGemma, fVLM-aligned BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART.

Interpretation limits in this chapter are concise: uneven organ support in chest CT and partial metric-domain mismatch for organ-decomposed text. A full validity discussion is provided in Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.
