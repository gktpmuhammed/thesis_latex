\chapter{Experiments and Results}\label{chapter:experiments}

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp_setup}
\subsection{Objectives and Research Questions}
This chapter evaluates organ-aware 3D CT report generation under a consistent \ac{CTRATE} protocol. Results are organized by four research questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Token-Interface Bundle):} Does the joint token-budget and interface design improve report quality?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Architecture):} Do heavier visual modules outperform the simpler masked cross-attention baseline?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Training Strategy):} How much do curriculum, hard-example, and imbalance policies change outcomes?
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Decoder Family):} Under the same frontend, how do MedGemma, BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART compare?
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Protocol}\label{sec:exp_eval_protocol}
\subsection{Metric Families}\label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
We report complementary metric groups. Lexical overlap is measured with BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}, \ac{ROUGEL}~\cite{lin2004rouge}, METEOR~\cite{banerjee2005meteor}, and \ac{CIDEr}~\cite{vedantam2015cider}. Semantic similarity is measured with \ac{BERTScore}~\cite{zhang2019bertscore}. Clinical and factual consistency is measured with \ac{CheXbert}~\cite{smit2020chexbert}, \ac{RadGraph}~\cite{jain2021radgraph}, \ac{GREEN}~\cite{ostmeier2024green}, \ac{RadCliQ}~\cite{yu2023radcliq}, and an SRR-BERT style score.
Here, \ac{CheXbert} is the report-labeling evaluator and should not be conflated with the CheXpert chest X-ray dataset~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} used in model pretraining pipelines.

Formal equations are provided in Appendix~\ref{appendix:metric_equations}. Chapter-level interpretation focuses on metric behavior and agreement patterns.

\subsection{Metric Interpretation Policy}\label{sec:metric_interpretation_policy}
Model ranking is anchored on \ac{GREEN} and \ac{RadGraph}, then cross-checked with lexical-semantic metrics. \ac{CheXbert} is reported but interpreted cautiously for organ-decomposed CT text because its design assumptions are closer to full-report label extraction workflows.
Unless noted otherwise, internal metric values in this thesis are reported on a $0$--$100$ scale.
\ac{RadCliQ} follows the inverse direction: lower values indicate better report quality.

For decoder-family analysis, we also report generation-behavior diagnostics to detect template collapse. These include exact-report uniqueness ratio, top-$k$ template coverage, and effective-template ratio $\exp(H)/N$, where $H$ is Shannon entropy over normalized report-frequency counts and $N$ is the number of generated reports.

\subsection{Evaluation Reproducibility and Result Provenance}
All checkpoints are evaluated with one standardized workflow: report generation, metric computation with the same RadEval framework~\cite{xu-etal-2025-radeval}, and structured aggregation of global and organ-level scores. This removes evaluation variability and keeps model comparisons methodologically consistent.

Metric definitions and table semantics are fixed before comparison. As a result, every experiment in this chapter is interpreted under the same scoring rules.

Quantitative claims are drawn only from official runs produced under this shared protocol. MedGemma ablations, text-policy comparisons, and decoder-family comparisons all follow the same data split, preprocessing assumptions, and evaluation pipeline.
Within each comparison block, decoding policy is also matched (beam/sampling and length constraints).

\subsection{Qualitative Protocol for Attention Maps}
Cross-attention maps are interpreted with the following checks: spatial overlap with the queried organ mask, compactness vs diffusion, stability across planes, and false activation in anatomically confounding neighbors.

\subsection{RQ1: Token-Interface Bundle}
RQ1 is answered with a strict token-count control inside the same stable-prefix branch. Base-8T outperforms Base-1T on \ac{GREEN}, BLEU-4, \ac{ROUGEL}, \ac{BERTScore}, and \ac{RadGraph}, but is worse on \ac{RadCliQ} (104.1 vs 100.2).

Because Base-1T and Base-8T share the same prefix/interface design and matched training policy, this is interpreted as a direct token-count effect under the current compute budget.

The mask-downsampling transition remains secondary: MaxPool-8T stays below Base-8T on the primary quality signals, so max-pooling alone does not explain the Base-8T gain.

\subsection{RQ2: Architecture Complexity}
Table~\ref{tab:delta_vs_baseline} summarizes deltas against baseline for representative models. In the maxpool-derived architecture branch, heavier modules such as Perceiver, multiscale, and CNN+alignment variants do not consistently outperform MaxPool-8T under the current training budget.

Within this branch, Multiscale-8T is the strongest of the heavier variants, but its gains are modest and not uniform across metric families. Perceiver-8T and CNNAlign-8T remain below MaxPool-8T on the main global signals, which weakens the claim that added architectural complexity is the main driver of quality in this setup.
A direct loss-composition comparison is also available in this branch: MaxPool-8T (pure LM objective) versus Align-8T (LM+alignment). Align-8T remains below MaxPool-8T on \ac{GREEN}, BLEU-4, and \ac{RadGraph}, while slightly better on \ac{RadCliQ} (100.7 vs 102.0), so alignment regularization alone does not deliver consistent global gains under the current setup.

The practical interpretation is that complexity helps only in narrow cases under the current budget and does not establish a robust global advantage. Under one-epoch training with the current optimization constraints, interface quality remains more reliable than heavier visual modules.

\subsection{RQ3: Training Strategy}
Training-policy interventions split into two branches. In the single-token area-derived branch, WeightedLoss-1T does not surpass Base-1T. In the maxpool-derived branch, HardMining-8T and Curriculum-8T do not surpass MaxPool-8T, and curriculum learning degrades most global metrics in the current two-phase configuration.

In the single-token area-derived branch, WeightedLoss-1T remains below Base-1T across clinical and lexical-semantic groups. This suggests that loss-side imbalance control alone does not recover baseline quality in the single-token branch.

In the maxpool-derived branch, HardMining-8T stays closer to MaxPool-8T but still remains below it on the primary quality signals. Curriculum-8T shows the largest overall regression in this branch. This likely reflects instability or underfitting under the current schedule rather than a generally negative verdict on curriculum ideas.
Interestingly, Curriculum-8T also yields the lowest \ac{RadCliQ} in the MedGemma training matrix, despite severe degradation on \ac{GREEN} and \ac{RadGraph}. Under the metric policy used in this thesis, this indicates a disagreement between \ac{RadCliQ} and the primary clinical ranking signals under this failure mode rather than evidence that Curriculum-8T is a stronger model overall. For this reason, \ac{RadCliQ} is not interpreted in isolation, and model ranking remains anchored on \ac{GREEN} and \ac{RadGraph}.

\subsection{RQ4: Decoder Family Comparison}
Decoder comparison is a core axis with fixed protocol and table schema. Table~\ref{tab:decoder_comparison} includes MedGemma, fVLM-aligned \ac{BERTbase}, \ac{GPTtwo}, and BioBART rows from the same standardized metric pass.

The current evidence shows a mixed but interpretable pattern. \ac{BERTbase} is strongest on \ac{GREEN}, \ac{RadGraph}, \ac{ROUGEL}, and \ac{BERTScore}, while \ac{GPTtwo} leads several overlap-style metrics including BLEU-4 and \ac{CIDEr} (and ties METEOR). BioBART is best on \ac{RadCliQ} in this table. MedGemma (Base-8T) remains competitive but trails \ac{BERTbase} on these aggregate metrics while showing much lower template reuse.

Table~\ref{tab:decoder_sanity} reports neutral run diagnostics such as sample count and average output length.

Table~\ref{tab:decoder_diversity} adds diversity diagnostics over normalized full reports. \ac{BERTbase} shows the strongest repetition profile among the four decoders: only $41.3\%$ unique reports, $19.0\%$ coverage by the top five templates, and $48.2\%$ coverage by the top fifty templates. MedGemma (Base-8T) shows much lower template reuse, with $100.0\%$ unique reports and only $3.2\%$ top-fifty coverage. Here, uniqueness is exact-match uniqueness on normalized full reports, so it quantifies template reuse after normalization rather than semantic similarity.

\section{Chapter Summary and Minimal Limitations Note}\label{sec:exp_summary}
The strongest MedGemma ablation under the current matched protocol is Base-8T when ranking is anchored on \ac{GREEN} and \ac{RadGraph}. In the full cross-decoder comparison (Table~\ref{tab:decoder_comparison}), \ac{BERTbase} is highest on both metrics. The result pattern across RQ1 to RQ3 indicates that token-count scaling from 1T to 8T improves quality in the stable-prefix branch, while heavier architectural modules and extra training-policy complexity provide limited additional gains. \ac{RadCliQ} is a notable exception and does not favor Base-8T in this ablation family.

The decoder axis for RQ4 has complete standardized rows for all targeted decoder families in this thesis: MedGemma, fVLM-aligned \ac{BERTbase}, \ac{GPTtwo}, and BioBART.

Interpretation limits in this chapter are concise: uneven organ support in chest CT and partial metric-domain mismatch for organ-decomposed text. A full validity discussion is provided in Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.
