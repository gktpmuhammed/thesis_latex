\chapter{Introduction}\label{chapter:introduction}
As deep learning has continued to advance over recent years, it has been applied to various fields of science and engineering. One of the most prominent applications of deep learning is in the field of computer vision. Deep learning models have achieved state-of-the-art performance on various computer vision tasks, such as image classification, object detection, and image segmentation.
However, deep learning models are data-hungry and require large amounts of labeled data to achieve high performance. This is especially true for medical imaging, where obtaining large-scale labeled datasets is challenging due to privacy concerns and the high cost of expert annotation. 

\section{Problem Statement}
Traditional NLP-based frameworks for radiology report generation often suffered from a lack of localized visual reasoning and a weak integration of clinical knowledge. While early 2D Vision-Language Models (VLMs) leveraging attention mechanisms improved the alignment between image regions and text tokens, they fundamentally failed to capture true three-dimensional anatomical context. In clinical practice, critical findings---such as lesion shape, spatial extent, and inter-slice continuity---depend entirely on volumetric data. 

Applying modern VLM architectures directly to 3D medical volumes (like CT scans) introduces substantial computational bottlenecks. The cubic growth of 3D attention mechanisms requires bridging architectures, such as perceptual resamplers or Q-Formers, to compress thousands of spatial tokens into a dense semantic representation without overwhelming the language decoder. Furthermore, while the field of generative AI has seen an expansion of capable multi-billion parameter foundation models, it remains an open question whether accurate medical report generation depends primarily on the 3D vision encoder or on the semantic reasoning capacity of the Large Language Model decoder.

\section{Thesis Formulation}
This thesis addresses these gaps by evaluating state-of-the-art 3D Vision-Language Modeling techniques for radiology report generation. Specifically, we investigate whether extracting single, highly semantic visual tokens per anatomical organ provides a cleaner signal to the LLM decoder than complex, multi-token representations.

To achieve this, we leverage an organ-guided decomposition methodology where reports are broken down anatomical region by region, enabling the VLM to perform targeted, localized reasoning. Our experiments are anchored on the \textbf{CT-RATE} dataset \cite{hamamci2024foundation}, a large-scale public dataset pairing 3D chest CT volumes with free-text radiology reports.

Instead of relying solely on traditional natural language metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) which often fail to capture clinical nuances---such as severely penalizing negative findings---this work assesses model performance using advanced clinical efficacy metrics like \textbf{RadGraph} \cite{jain2021radgraph} and \textbf{GREEN} (Generative Radiology Report Evaluation and Error Notation) \cite{ostmeier2024green}, ensuring generated reports are clinically actionable rather than linguistically mimetic.

By running extensive ablations across decoder architectures---comparing medically aligned causal language models (e.g., \textbf{MedGemma} \cite{yang2024medgemma}) against generic causal models (\textbf{GPT-2}) and encoder-decoder architectures (\textbf{BioBART})---this research analyzes how decoder choice affects mapping sparse 3D visual tokens into structured clinical text.

\section{Contributions}
The main contributions of this thesis are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive 3D VLM Benchmarking}: We conduct one of the first extensive comparative analysis of decoder architectures (causal vs. encoder-decoder) coupled with 3D vision encoders for volumetric report generation.
    \item \textbf{Organ-Guided Tokenization Strategy}: We show that compressing high-density 3D spatial features into a single, organ-specific visual token (via cross-attention) outperforms more complex multi-token bridging architectures in our current ablations.
    \item \textbf{Clinical Efficacy Prioritization}: We validate generated results strictly against clinical-factuality metrics (GREEN, RadGraph) to isolate true clinical reasoning from rote n-gram memorization.
\end{enumerate}
