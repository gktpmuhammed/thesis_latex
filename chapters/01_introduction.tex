\chapter{Introduction}\label{chapter:introduction}
Deep learning has become an important tool across science and engineering, with one of its strongest impacts in computer vision. Today, models perform very well on tasks such as image classification, object detection, and segmentation. These results, however, usually depend on large labeled datasets. In medical imaging, building such datasets is difficult because patient data must be handled carefully and expert annotation is costly and time-consuming.

Within medical imaging, radiology report generation is a high-impact task because reports are the main communication channel between image analysis and clinical decision-making. A useful model must do more than produce fluent text. It must describe findings accurately, preserve anatomical context, and avoid clinically misleading statements. This makes the problem closer to structured clinical reasoning than to generic image captioning.

\section{Problem Statement}
Traditional NLP-based frameworks for radiology report generation often suffered from a lack of localized visual reasoning and a weak integration of clinical knowledge. While early 2D Vision-Language Models (VLMs) leveraging attention mechanisms improved the alignment between image regions and text tokens, they fundamentally failed to capture true three-dimensional anatomical context. In clinical practice, critical findings such as lesion shape, spatial extent, and inter-slice continuity depend entirely on volumetric data. 

Applying modern VLM architectures directly to 3D medical volumes, like CT scans, introduces substantial computational bottlenecks. The cubic growth of 3D attention mechanisms requires bridging architectures, such as perceptual resamplers or Q-Formers, to compress thousands of spatial tokens into a dense semantic representation without overwhelming the language decoder. Furthermore, while the field of generative AI has seen an expansion of capable multi-billion parameter foundation models, it remains an open question whether accurate medical report generation depends primarily on the 3D vision encoder or on the semantic reasoning capacity of the Large Language Model decoder.

\section{Thesis Formulation}
This thesis builds on fVLM~\cite{shui2025fvlm} and focuses on radiology report generation from 3D chest CT. Rather than introducing a new vision backbone, we reuse the pretrained fVLM vision encoder and study how to turn its visual features into clinically reasonable reports.

Our main objective is to improve report quality through design choices around this encoder. Since fVLM does not release report-generation weights and reports limited decoder details, we implement a BERT-base branch \cite{devlin2019bert} with language modeling loss on CT-RATE as a reproducible internal reference aligned with the paper description. We then evaluate additional generation strategies, including organ-conditioned token interfaces and decoder adaptation. Experiments are conducted on the CT-RATE dataset \cite{hamamci2024foundation}, a large-scale public dataset pairing 3D chest CT volumes with free-text radiology reports.

Instead of relying only on text-overlap metrics such as BLEU \cite{papineni2002bleu} and ROUGE \cite{lin2004rouge}, we also evaluate clinical quality using RadGraph \cite{jain2021radgraph} and GREEN \cite{ostmeier2024green}, the Generative Radiology Report Evaluation and Error Notation metric. This helps us judge whether the generated reports are clinically useful, not only lexically similar.

Through ablations across decoder architectures, including MedGemma \cite{yang2024medgemma}, BERT-base \cite{devlin2019bert}, GPT-2 \cite{radford2019language}, and BioBART \cite{yuan2022biobart}, we analyze which decoder choices and interface designs best convert pretrained 3D visual representations into structured clinical text.

\section{Contributions}
The main contributions of this thesis are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{fVLM-Based Report Generation Framework}: We build an end-to-end CT report generation pipeline around a pretrained fVLM vision encoder, with a shared organ-aware visual frontend, decoder-side adaptation, and a reproducible BERT-base reference branch.
    \item \textbf{Systematic Quality-Improvement Study}: We evaluate practical strategies to improve report generation quality, including organ-conditioned token interfaces, decoder choices such as MedGemma, BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART, and training ablations under a controlled setup.
    \item \textbf{Clinical Efficacy Prioritization}: We validate generated results strictly against clinical-factuality metrics such as GREEN, RadGraph to distinguish genuine clinical correctness from superficial overlap-based gains driven by memorized reporting patterns.
\end{enumerate}
