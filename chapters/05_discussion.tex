\chapter{Discussion}\label{chapter:discussion}

This chapter interprets the evidence from Chapter~\ref{chapter:experiments}. The goal is to explain why the observed ranking emerged, which conclusions are robust, and where claims should be bounded.

\section{Why Interface and Token Count Dominated}
Empirical signal. Within MedGemma ablations under the matched protocol, Base-8T is strongest on the primary global metrics and improves GREEN, RadGraph, BLEU-4, ROUGE-L, and BERTScore over Base-1T, but not RadCliQ.
For this pair, the gain can be interpreted as a token-count effect because Base-1T and Base-8T share the same stable-prefix interface and training-policy setup. This is a within-MedGemma result and is separate from the cross-decoder ranking in Table~\ref{tab:decoder_comparison}.

Mechanistic interpretation. A plausible explanation is that stable interface components reduce ambiguity at the visual-text boundary:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item explicit visual token delimitation (sentinel wrapping),
    \item consistent positional encoding over visual prefix tokens,
    \item stabilized projection into decoder embedding scale.
\end{enumerate}
Together they reduce ambiguity between visual context and textual instruction/response tokens. With these controls fixed, the observed Base-1T to Base-8T gain indicates that higher token capacity better preserves organ-level detail under the current budget.

Negative ablations in context. Perceiver-style resampling and multiscale fusion are not intrinsically weak. Under the current low effective batch, long context, and short training budget, their additional complexity likely increases optimization burden more than it improves report-level factuality.
Within the same maxpool lineage, adding alignment regularization alone (Align-8T) also does not outperform the pure-LM reference (MaxPool-8T), which is consistent with the RQ2 interpretation.

Curriculum and hard-example strategies also underperform the best stable-prefix baseline. This indicates that weighting/sampling can refine learning but does not substitute for strong token-interface capacity.

Case-study support. The head-averaged overlays in Figure~\ref{fig:attention_mean_valid730} provide spatial evidence consistent with metric trends. In this representative patient, Base-8T shows higher in-mask concentration than Multiscale and CNN-alignment variants for both lung (49.4\% vs 36.5\% and 44.8\%) and kidney (28.5\% vs 14.9\% and 15.6\%). The larger separation in kidney also fits the long-tail difficulty of sparse organs. These qualitative maps are used as supporting evidence, not as standalone proof.

Design implication. For this task family, the practical order is to stabilize the visual-language interface first, verify organ grounding, and then scale architectural complexity.

\section{Metric Disagreement and Interpretation}
Different metric families reward different behavior, so disagreement is expected:
\begin{itemize}
    \item lexical metrics reward phrasing overlap,
    \item embedding metrics reward semantic proximity,
    \item clinical metrics reward factual and relational correctness.
\end{itemize}

The decoder results illustrate this directly. BERT-base is strongest on GREEN, RadGraph, ROUGE-L, and BERTScore, while GPT-2 leads several overlap-style metrics including BLEU-4 and CIDEr (and ties METEOR). BioBART is best on RadCliQ and BERT-base is worst on RadCliQ. CheXbert remains useful but is not treated as the sole clinical proxy for organ-decomposed CT text. Consistent with Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_metrics}, model ranking prioritizes GREEN and RadGraph, then checks lexical-semantic coherence.

The MedGemma training ablations show that metric disagreement is not limited to decoder-family trade-offs. Curriculum-8T performs poorly on the primary clinical metrics GREEN and RadGraph, yet attains the lowest RadCliQ among the MedGemma rows. This suggests that RadCliQ can disagree sharply with the primary clinical ranking under certain failure modes. Accordingly, in this thesis RadCliQ is treated as a complementary diagnostic metric rather than a standalone ranking criterion.

Diversity diagnostics add an important correction to aggregate metric ranking. In Table~\ref{tab:decoder_diversity}, BERT-base has high template reuse, with almost half of reports covered by its top fifty normalized templates. MedGemma (Base-8T) shows substantially lower reuse, with near-complete report uniqueness under normalized matching. This explains why BERT can score well on global overlap-style metrics while still producing less case-specific report content.

\section{Generalization and Organ Coverage}
The chest-CT setting yields strong thoracic support and sparse support for several upper-abdominal organs. This long-tail distribution affects both training dynamics and evaluation variance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item sparse organs have noisier metrics,
    \item frequent thoracic patterns dominate global optimization,
    \item template bias risk increases for low-support organs.
\end{itemize}
Accordingly, organ-level conclusions are most reliable for high-support organs and should be interpreted cautiously for very sparse ones.
The lung/kidney attention comparison is consistent with this pattern: differences are visible in both organs, but sparse-organ behavior is more sensitive and should be validated over more than one case.

\section{Decoder Family Axis}
The decoder-comparison protocol is structurally fixed with a shared frontend, shared metric pipeline, and shared artifact policy. All target rows are now complete under this protocol: MedGemma, fVLM-aligned BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART. The current evidence favors BERT-base on GREEN, RadGraph, ROUGE-L, and BERTScore, while GPT-2 leads overlap-style metrics such as BLEU-4 and CIDEr; RadCliQ is the explicit exception where BioBART is stronger.

For practical deployment under organ-aware reporting objectives, model choice depends on priority: BERT-base is stronger on aggregate metric ranking, while MedGemma is safer when lower template repetition and higher case-specific variation are prioritized.
In short, aggregate GREEN/RadGraph-centered ranking favors BERT-base, diversity/repetition diagnostics favor MedGemma, RadCliQ favors BioBART, and the preferred decoder should be selected by deployment objective rather than a single universal ``best'' label.

\section{Relation to Prior Paradigms}
Compared with many 2D report-generation settings, 3D CT introduces higher token pressure and stronger grounding demands. The results suggest that decoder scaling or heavier visual modules alone are insufficient if token semantics at the interface are not explicit and stable. This aligns with anatomy-guided trends in recent segmentation-aware and decomposition-aware literature.

\section{Validity and Limits}
Internal validity is strongest for MedGemma-family comparisons because data interfaces and evaluation pipelines are controlled. Decoder-family conclusions are now grounded by completed MedGemma, fVLM-aligned BERT-base, GPT-2, and BioBART rows under one protocol.
For RQ1 specifically, causal attribution to token count is stronger than in earlier drafts because Base-1T and Base-8T now share matched stable-prefix structure; remaining uncertainty mainly comes from single-run variance.

Findings are specific to organ-decomposed chest-CT reporting and should not be over-generalized to other modalities or report styles without controlled replication.

Construct validity is strengthened by metric bundles plus case studies, but still depends on imperfect automatic metrics. Human expert evaluation remains outside the current chapter scope.

\section{Practical Implications and Synthesis}
For practitioners, the evidence supports an interface-first recipe:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item use explicit organ-conditioned supervision,
    \item stabilize visual prefix semantics and projection scale,
    \item run lightweight ablations before heavy architectural scaling,
    \item use metric bundles plus qualitative spatial audits,
    \item separate protocol-matched internal comparisons from external contextual baselines.
\end{enumerate}

Overall synthesis: under realistic compute constraints, interface calibration is the dominant success factor in this organ-aware 3D CT report-generation setup.
