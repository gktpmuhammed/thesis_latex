\section{Vision Transformers in Medical Imaging}\label{sec:rw_vit}
%% ========================================================

The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image} fundamentally shifted the paradigm of computer vision by applying the transformer architecture, originally designed for natural language processing, to image recognition. ViT divides an input image into fixed-size non-overlapping patches, projects each patch into an embedding, and processes the resulting sequence through a standard transformer encoder. By treating image patches as tokens and relying entirely on multi-head self-attention, ViT eliminates the need for convolutional inductive biases (such as translation invariance and local receptive fields) and achieves competitive or superior performance compared to Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) when trained on massive datasets like ImageNet.

\subsection{Architectural Evolutions: Hierarchical and Self-Supervised ViTs}

While the original ViT required large-scale supervised pretraining, subsequent architectures addressed its limitations. The Swin Transformer~\cite{liu2021swin} introduced a hierarchical structure with shifted windows, computing self-attention locally within non-overlapping windows that shift across layers. This hierarchical design reintroduces some useful inductive biases, making it highly efficient for dense prediction tasks like segmentation and object detection, which are critical in medical imaging contexts where precise anatomical boundaries matter.

Simultaneously, advancements in self-supervised learning alleviated the reliance on massive labeled datasets, a severe bottleneck in the medical domain. Masked Autoencoders (MAE)~\cite{he2022masked} proposed an asymmetric encoder-decoder architecture where the encoder operates only on an unmasked subset of patches (e.g., 25\%), and a lightweight decoder reconstructs the heavily masked (75\%) original image. This approach enables the learning of robust, high-level visual representations directly from unlabeled data. Building upon self-supervised paradigms, DINOv2~\cite{oquab2023dinov2} demonstrated that models trained via self-distillation with no labels can learn universal visual features that are highly transferable across diverse domains, capturing part-level semantic correspondences without explicit supervision.

\subsection{Adaptation to 3D Medical Volumes}

In the medical imaging domain, transformers have been widely adopted to capture the long-range dependencies crucial for understanding complex anatomical structures. Early adaptations like TransUNet~\cite{chen2021transunet} combined a CNN encoder for local feature extraction with a transformer bottleneck for global context in 2D medical image segmentation.

However, clinical data such as Computed Tomography (CT) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) are inherently 3D volumetric scans. Processing these volume slice-by-slice discards critical through-plane spatial context. UNETR~\cite{hatamizadeh2022unetr} addressed this by using a ViT encoder to process 3D volumetric data directly, extracting 3D patches from the input volume. 

The transition from 2D image patches to 3D volumetric patches introduces substantial computational cost. In a standard self-attention mechanism, memory and computational complexity grow quadratically with sequence length. For a 3D medical image, sequence length expands cubically with respect to spatial dimensions. If a high-resolution CT scan is embedded into thousands of 3D tokens, global self-attention can exceed the memory limits of modern GPUs. To mitigate this, modern 3D Vision Transformers commonly use sparse attention, shifted-window variants, or early spatial downsampling.

\section{Vision--Language Models}\label{sec:rw_vlm}
%% ========================================================

Vision--language models (VLMs) aim to bridge the semantic gap between visual perception and natural language understanding. These models learn joint representations of images and text, enabling a wide array of cross-modal tasks such as image captioning, visual question answering (VQA), and complex multi-modal reasoning.

\subsection{Contrastive Alignment and the CLIP Paradigm}

CLIP (Contrastive Language--Image Pre-training)~\cite{radford2021learning} introduced a highly scalable approach to learning visual representations from natural language supervision. By training independent vision and text encoders to maximize the cosine similarity of matching image--text pairs in a shared embedding space while minimizing the similarity of millions of non-matching pairs, CLIP learns highly transferable visual concepts. Trained on 400 million image--text pairs curated from the internet, CLIP demonstrates strong zero-shot transfer capabilities across a wide range of downstream visual tasks.

Recent advances have refined the CLIP objective. SigLIP (Sigmoid Loss for Language Image Pre-Training)~\cite{zhai2023siglip} replaces the standard softmax-based contrastive loss with a pairwise sigmoid loss. This change removes the need for global views of all negative pairs across devices, improving training efficiency and scalability and often yielding stronger representations. The contrastive learning paradigm established by CLIP and extended by SigLIP remains a common foundation for aligning vision and language modalities in modern VLMs.

\subsection{Generative VLMs and Open-Weight Ecosystems}

While contrastive models focus purely on alignment and retrieval, generative VLMs extend the paradigm to produce natural language outputs explicitly conditioned on visual inputs. BLIP (Bootstrapping Language-Image Pre-training)~\cite{li2022blip} proposed a unified framework that jointly trains a contrastive alignment objective, an image--text matching classification objective, and a generative language modeling objective. This multi-task design enables BLIP to be highly effective for both comprehensive image understanding and robust text generation tasks.

Building upon this, BLIP-2~\cite{li2023blip2} introduced a compute-efficient strategy for bridging the modality gap: the Q-Former (Query Transformer). The Q-Former acts as an informational bottleneck between a frozen image encoder and a frozen large language model. It utilizes a set of learnable query vectors that cross-attend to the dense visual features extracted by the image encoder, forcing the queries to extract only the most text-relevant visual information. By doing so, the Q-Former compresses thousands of spatial visual tokens into a fixed, small number of semantic latent tokens (e.g., 32 tokens). These compressed tokens are then linearly projected directly into the text embedding space of the frozen LLM. This architecture demonstrated that one can achieve strong multi-modal capabilities without unfreezing the massive weights of the underlying language or vision foundation models, reducing computational cost.

A similar design philosophy for handling high-density visual signals was adopted by DeepMind's Flamingo~\cite{alayrac2022flamingo}. Rather than a Q-Former, Flamingo utilized a Perceiver Resampler mechanism, which also cross-attends to a large input array (such as a high-resolution image or a varying-length video) using a small set of latent, learnable query vectors. Flamingo then interleaved these compressed visual tokens with standard text tokens inside a frozen LLM through gated cross-attention layers, demonstrating strong few-shot learning and in-context reasoning capabilities.

LLaVA (Large Language-and-Vision Assistant)~\cite{liu2024visual} vastly simplified these complex bridging architectures by proving that a straightforward Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) projection layer could effectively map visual features directly from a pretrained CLIP encoder into the native vocabulary embedding space of an LLM, entirely discarding the need for Q-Formers or Resamplers. Through visual instruction tuning (training the model on instruction-following datasets combining images with conversational text), LLaVA achieved remarkable multi-modal dialogue capabilities. Recently, open-source initiatives have rapidly accelerated VLM research. Models like InternVL~\cite{chen2023internvl} focus on massive scaling of the vision foundation model to better align with generic linguistic tasks, while the Molmo family~\cite{deitke2024molmo} emphasizes the crucial role of highly curated, completely open-source point-based multimodal data to achieve state-of-the-art results with highly efficient projector architectures.

\subsection{Medical Vision-Language Models}

Adapting VLMs to the medical domain presents significant unique challenges. Medical images (such as high-resolution multi-parametric MRIs or chest X-rays) have fundamentally different characteristics from natural images (like those in COCO/ImageNet). Furthermore, clinical texts utilize a dense, highly specialized vocabulary, and tolerance for error in medical AI is low.

Early attempts to bridge this gap focused on domain-specific contrastive pretraining. BiomedCLIP~\cite{zhang2023biomedclip} addressed this by pretraining a CLIP-style architecture from scratch on 15 million biomedical image--text pairs sourced from PubMed Central articles, demonstrating that in-domain pretraining improves performance on downstream biomedical classification and retrieval tasks compared to generic CLIP models.

For 2D clinical radiology, development has accelerated in recent years~\cite{wang2025rrgsurvey}. CheXagent~\cite{chen2024chexagent} was introduced as a clinical foundation model for chest X-ray interpretation, fine-tuned on large corpora of radiology reports to interpret images and answer clinical questions. MAIRA-2~\cite{bannur2024maira} advanced grounded report generation by linking generated assertions to localized bounding boxes in the original X-ray, reducing hallucinations and improving trustworthiness.

For 3D medical imaging, the challenge is compounded by the sheer size of the volumetric data and the scarcity of paired text data. Hamamci et al.~\cite{hamamci2024foundation} introduced CT-CLIP, a contrastive learning framework specifically engineered for 3D computed tomography. Trained exclusively on the massive CT-RATE dataset, which pairs unannotated 3D volumetric CT scans directly with their corresponding free-text radiology reports, CT-CLIP successfully learns 3D structural visual representations that map directly to the clinical text manifold. This foundational work represents one of the first successful large-scale efforts to apply multi-modal vision--language pretraining directly to unstructured volumetric medical data.

MedGemma~\cite{yang2024medgemma} is a family of medical foundation models built on top of Google's rigorous Gemma architecture~\cite{team2024gemma}. MedGemma structurally inherits the robust capabilities of the general-purpose Gemma model but undergoes rigorous continued pretraining and specialized instruction-tuning on vast troves of verified biomedical literature, medical board exams, and de-identified clinical notes. The resulting instruction-tuned variant (MedGemma-4B-IT) demonstrates exceptional zero-shot and few-shot capability on complex medical reasoning tasks, making it a highly compelling, medically-aligned language decoder for complex medical VLM pipelines, and ultimately serving as one of the core generative components evaluated in this thesis.

\section{Decoders and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning}\label{sec:rw_llm}
%% ========================================================

The generation of complex radiology reports from visual features requires a demonstrably powerful language decoder. The development of natural language generation models has largely bifurcated into two fundamentally distinct architectural paradigms: Encoder-Decoder models and Causal Language Models. Understanding the mechanistic differences between these paradigms is crucial when selecting a backbone for medical vision-language reasoning.

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Architectures vs. Causal Modeling}

Encoder-Decoder models, such as BART (Bidirectional and Auto-Regressive Transformers)~\cite{lewis2019bart} and T5, utilize a bidirectional encoder to process the input context comprehensively and an autoregressive decoder to generate the output sequence token by token. Because the encoder can attend to both left and right context simultaneously during the prompt processing phase, these models excel at tasks requiring deep semantic understanding of the entire input condition before generating text (e.g., summarization or machine translation). BART is specifically pretrained by corrupting documents (via token masking, deletion, and sentence permutation) and optimizing a reconstruction loss.

In the biomedical domain, BioBART~\cite{yuan2022biobart} adapts this exact architecture by continually pretraining the baseline BART model on a massive corpus of PubMed abstracts. This domain-specific pretraining allows BioBART to inherit the robust grammatical generation capabilities of BART while rigidly aligning its vocabulary and internal latent representations with complex, highly specialized medical terminology. In standard vision-language pipelines utilizing Encoder-Decoder backbones, visual embeddings are typically fed directly into the bidirectional encoder, allowing the system to build a rich, globally aware contextual representation before the autoregressive generation phase even begins.

Conversely, Causal Language Models (CLMs) utilize a decoder-only architecture that predicts the next token in a sequence strictly based on preceding leftward tokens. GPT-2 (Generative Pre-trained Transformer 2)~\cite{radford2019language} popularized this highly scalable paradigm, demonstrating that simple unsupervised next-token prediction on diverse web corpora could intuitively yield models capable of coherent, zero-shot, open-ended text generation. Due to its relatively modest scale (e.g., 124M parameters for the base model) and exceptionally clean architectural foundation, GPT-2 remains a popular, tractable baseline for experimental diagnostic text generation tasks where compute is constrained.

Unlike bidirectional encoders, causal models process the conditioning prompt (including visual tokens prepended as a ``soft prompt'') autoregressively. While theoretically limiting the deep bidirectional mingling of context tokens, the massive parameter count of modern causal models overrides this limitation through sheer representational capacity. 

Recent foundation models have scaled the decoder-only paradigm to tens of billions of parameters. The LLaMA family~\cite{touvron2023llama} and the Gemma family~\cite{team2024gemma} have demonstrated strong capabilities in generalized natural language understanding, logical reasoning, and instruction-following. MedGemma~\cite{yang2024medgemma}, a medical variant (e.g., MedGemma-4B-IT), extends these capabilities with continued medical pretraining and instruction tuning. In causal VLM architectures, preserving the spatial and semantic integrity of the visual token prefix is important because downstream generation depends directly on this initial visual context.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)}

Adapting massive decoders (especially multi-billion parameter foundation models like MedGemma) to specialized downstream tasks such as highly localized, organ-specific radiology report generation via full fine-tuning is computationally prohibitive and can degrade the base model's general linguistic capabilities. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods address this by updating only a small fraction of the model's parameters while keeping most pretrained weights frozen.

Early PEFT strategies included Adapter modules~\cite{houlsby2019parameter}, which introduced small, trainable feed-forward bottleneck networks sandwiched between the frozen multi-head attention and feed-forward transformer sub-layers. Another approach, Prefix-Tuning~\cite{li2021prefix}, mathematically optimizes a sequence of continuous task-specific prompt vectors prepended to the transformer layers. While effective in reducing forgetting, these methods alter the network topology, often introducing additional inference latency or reducing the available context window depth.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)~\cite{hu2022lora} addresses these issues and is now widely adopted in foundation-model fine-tuning. LoRA assumes that required updates to dense weight matrices have low intrinsic rank. It introduces trainable low-rank matrices in parallel with frozen linear layers (commonly query and value projections in self-attention). Instead of updating the full matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$, LoRA learns smaller matrices $A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ where $r \ll \min(d, k)$, yielding $W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + B A$. Because $B A$ can be merged into $W_0$ for deployment, LoRA does not add extra inference latency after merging.

To further reduce training cost, QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} combines LoRA with 4-bit Normal Float (NF4) double quantization. In QLoRA, the frozen pretrained model weights reside in 4-bit precision, while only the lightweight, trainable LoRA adapter matrices are cast to higher precision (e.g., BFloat16) during backpropagation. This combination substantially reduces VRAM requirements for fine-tuning multi-billion parameter foundation models, allowing faster iteration on standard hardware.

\section{Radiology Report Generation}\label{sec:rw_reportgen}
%% ========================================================

Automatic radiology report generation is the task of producing clinically accurate free-text reports from medical images. This task is fundamentally more challenging than generic image captioning because generated reports must be factually accurate, cover all relevant pathological findings (and explicitly note the absence of others), and strictly adhere to dense clinical conventions.

\subsection{Traditional and Early Transformer Approaches}

Early approaches to radiology report generation adapted encoder--decoder architectures directly from the natural image captioning literature. Jing et al.~\cite{jing2018automatic} proposed a co-attention mechanism combined with a hierarchical LSTM decoder to generate structured medical reports from chest X-rays. However, these CNN--RNN frameworks typically produced generic, repetitive descriptions and struggled significantly with capturing rare or subtle findings, often suffering from severe data imbalance in clinical datasets.

Transformer-based methods subsequently improved report generation quality by better tracking long-term context. Chen et al.~\cite{chen2020generating} introduced a memory-driven transformer that used a relational memory module to record key clinical information during autoregressive generation, markedly improving the coherence of long reports. In a follow-up, Chen et al.~\cite{chen2022crossmodal} proposed cross-modal memory networks that maintained separate but interacting memory banks for visual and textual features, enabling more robust cross-modal reasoning. Demonstrating the value of initialization, Nicolson et al.~\cite{nicolson2023improving} showed that warm-starting both the vision encoder (e.g., from ImageNet or CheXpert) and the language decoder (e.g., from clinical BERT) substantially improved chest X-ray report generation over training from scratch.

\subsection{The Evaluation Paradigm: From N-Grams to Clinical Efficacy}

Early report-generation studies relied heavily on lexical overlap metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR. In medical reporting, lexical overlap alone is insufficient because clinically meaningful errors can occur with small wording changes. The field therefore shifted toward clinically grounded metrics such as CheXbert~\cite{smit2020chexbert}, RadGraph~\cite{jain2021radgraph}, and GREEN~\cite{ostmeier2024green}, which better reflect factual and relational correctness. In this thesis, the high-level motivation is introduced here, while the operational metric interpretation policy is defined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_metrics}.

\subsection{2D Radiology Foundation Models}

As the field moved toward the foundation model paradigm, researchers began adapting large, frozen language models as report decoders. R2GenGPT~\cite{wang2023r2gengpt} demonstrated that a frozen LLM combined with a trainable visual encoder and a lightweight projection layer could generate high-quality radiology reports with competitive performance on both NLG and clinical efficacy metrics. This paradigm benefits directly from the language understanding capabilities and medical knowledge embedded in pretrained LLMs while keeping multi-modal training costs manageable.

Recently, the field has seen the emergence of true foundation models specifically tailored for 2D X-ray interpretation. CheXagent~\cite{chen2024chexagent} stands out as a pioneering clinical foundation model designed to interpret chest X-rays. By fine-tuning a massive VLM on diverse corpora of radiology reports and visual question-answering pairs, CheXagent achieved state-of-the-art performance in both free-text report generation and interactive clinical dialogue. 

Simultaneously, the architectural focus shifted from mere linguistic fluency to rigorous clinical grounding. MAIRA-2~\cite{bannur2024maira} introduced the concept of grounded radiology report generation. Instead of hallucinating plausible text based on global image context, MAIRA-2 explicitly maps generated clinical assertions in the text back to localized bounding boxes in the original X-ray. This spatial grounding dramatically reduces clinical hallucinations, a critical safety requirement for medical deployment. 

\subsection{3D Volumetric Report Generation}

While 2D X-ray interpretation has matured rapidly, 3D volumetric report generation (e.g., from CT or MRI scans) remains an open and highly challenging frontier. The jump from 2D to 3D exponentially increases the computational complexity of the visual token sequence. Furthermore, until the recent release of the CT-RATE dataset~\cite{hamamci2024foundation}, there was a severe scarcity of paired 3D volumes and free-text reports available for training.

The release of CT-RATE spurred the rapid development of several pioneering 3D multimodal foundation models:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CT2Rep}~\cite{hamamci2024ct2rep}: One of the earliest works to leverage the CT-RATE dataset, CT2Rep demonstrated that automated radiology report generation for 3D medical imaging is feasible by properly projecting 3D visual tokens into a robust language decoder.
    \item \textbf{M3D}~\cite{bai2024m3d}: Advancing 3D medical image analysis with Multi-Modal Large Language Models, M3D proposed strategies to efficiently compress volumetric data into spatial tokens, enabling the LLM to process entire CT scans without exceeding context windows.
    \item \textbf{Argus}~\cite{argus2024}: Argus introduced comprehensive benchmarking and enhancement strategies for VLMs in 3D radiology, testing models ranging from 3B configurations to massive 70B parameter deployments. Argus highlighted that scaling the LLM capacity yields direct improvements in clinical efficacy metrics like GREEN.
    \item \textbf{Med3DVLM}~\cite{med3dvlm2024} and \textbf{RadFM}~\cite{radfm2024}: Both models established unified 3D vision-language frameworks, focusing on cross-modal alignment between highly unstructured 3D voxel spaces and precise clinical text.
\end{itemize}

Recent work has also emphasized fine-grained anatomical supervision for CT understanding. fVLM~\cite{shui2025fvlm} introduces large-scale and fine-grained vision-language pretraining for CT and reports strong gains on report generation benchmarks. Its organ-aware supervision and decomposition perspective directly motivate the organ-guided decomposition strategy used in this thesis.

A key challenge unifying these works is token compression. Because a raw 3D CT volume contains vastly more information than a 2D image, naive patch extraction results in tens of thousands of tokens that can overwhelm the LLM decoder. Recent studies~\cite{hamamci2025bettertokensbetter3d} emphasize that tokenization strategies (such as Perceiver-based latent resampling, spatial 3D pooling, or learned region-of-interest selection) are an important factor for effective 3D VLM performance.

\section{3D Medical Image Segmentation and Organ-Guided Attention}\label{sec:rw_3dseg}
%% ========================================================

Accurate anatomical localization is an essential prerequisite for generating highly specific, clinically actionable radiology reports. The availability of robust, automated whole-body 3D segmentation tools has enabled novel VLM architectures that explicitly leverage anatomical priors to ground their text generation.

\subsection{Volumetric Segmentation Frameworks}

While early segmentation models relied on 3D CNNs (e.g., 3D U-Net), transformer-based architectures have increasingly dominated this space by effectively capturing long-range contextual dependencies. TransUNet~\cite{chen2021transunet} and Swin-UNet~\cite{cao2023swin} demonstrated the efficacy of transformers for 2D medical segmentation, but volumetric data requires dedicated 3D processing. 

nnFormer (Interleaved Transformer for Volumetric Medical Image Segmentation)~\cite{zhou2021nnformer} extends transformer capabilities explicitly to 3D medical images. By interleaving local 3D convolution operations with global multi-head self-attention mechanisms, nnFormer captures both the intricate local textures of tumors and the global geometrical relationships of large organs, setting strong benchmarks across multiple 3D medical datasets.

In practical clinical pipelines, TotalSegmentator~\cite{wasserthal2023totalsegmentator} has emerged as an indispensable, widely used utility for automatic segmentation. Built upon the robust nnU-Net framework, TotalSegmentator provides highly reliable, out-of-the-box segmentation of 104 distinct anatomical structures, including major organs, bones, vessels, and muscles, from standard CT images. The discrete 3D masks produced by such algorithms can serve directly as precise anatomical priors for downstream generative tasks.

\subsection{Organ-Guided Attention and Clinical Grounding}

Incorporating these anatomical priors into VLM architectures enables a shift from global image captioning to localized, organ-specific reasoning. Rather than processing an entire CT volume as a uniform sequence of visual tokens, organ-guided mechanisms restrict or bias the model's cross-attention to regions corresponding to specific query structures (e.g., the lungs or the liver).

This approach draws heavily upon learnable query architectures such as the Perceiver~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver}, where a fixed set of latent vectors attends to a massive sensory input. In the medical domain, these latent vectors can represent specific organs. By masking the visual features using 3D segmentation maps prior to cross-attention, the language decoder is forced to focus only on the relevant anatomy when generating the corresponding sentence in the radiology report. 

Furthermore, in the context of multi-task learning~\cite{caruana1997multitask}, organ-specific feature extraction can be significantly enhanced via auxiliary classification objectives. By jointly training the visual encoder to perform specific disease classification tasks alongside open-ended report generation, the model learns a more disentangled and discriminative organ-level representation. This auxiliary grounding acts as a powerful inductive bias, encouraging the visual encoder to capture the subtle physiological abnormalities that drive accurate clinical reporting.
