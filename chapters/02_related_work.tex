\chapter{Related Work}\label{chapter:related_work}

Building on the problem framing in Chapter~\ref{chapter:introduction}, this chapter reviews prior work that directly motivates our design choices for 3D chest CT report generation. The discussion follows the same technical axis used in this thesis: visual encoding for volumetric data, vision--language modeling, decoder adaptation, report generation and evaluation, and organ-guided anatomical grounding.

%% ========================================================
\section{Vision Transformers}\label{sec:rw_vit}
%% ========================================================

The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image} introduced a transformer-first approach to image analysis. It splits an image into fixed-size non-overlapping patches, projects each patch into an embedding, and processes the resulting token sequence with a standard transformer encoder. In contrast to CNNs, ViT does not rely on hard-coded convolutional priors such as local receptive fields or translation invariance. Instead, spatial relations are learned through self-attention. With sufficient large-scale pretraining, this formulation achieved competitive or better performance than strong CNN baselines on standard vision benchmarks.

\subsection{Architectural Evolutions: Hierarchical and Self-Supervised ViTs}

After ViT, transformer variants sought to address its limitations for dense vision tasks and training efficiency. The Swin Transformer~\cite{liu2021swin} introduced hierarchical feature maps and window-based self-attention, with shifted windows across layers to exchange information between local regions. This design lowered computational cost while preserving strong representational capacity. As a result, transformer backbones became more practical for detection and segmentation tasks, which are important in medical imaging.

In parallel, self-supervised learning reduced dependence on expensive annotations. Masked Autoencoders (MAE)~\cite{he2022masked} use an asymmetric encoder-decoder setup: the encoder processes only visible patches, while a lightweight decoder reconstructs masked content, often with masking ratios around 75\%. This strategy learns strong visual representations directly from unlabeled images. DINOv2~\cite{oquab2023dinov2} further showed that self-distillation without labels can produce robust and transferable features across many downstream tasks.

\subsection{Adaptation to 3D Medical Volumes}

In medical imaging, transformers are attractive because they capture long-range spatial dependencies that are difficult to model with purely local operators. Early hybrid designs such as TransUNet~\cite{chen2021transunet} combined CNN-based local feature extraction with transformer-based global context modeling for 2D segmentation.

Clinical modalities such as CT and MRI are inherently volumetric. Slice-by-slice processing can miss through-plane continuity and weak cross-slice patterns. UNETR~\cite{hatamizadeh2022unetr} addressed this limitation by tokenizing 3D patches and encoding the full volume with a ViT backbone.

The main limitation of direct 3D transformer modeling is computational cost. Attention complexity grows quadratically with token count, while token count in volumetric inputs grows rapidly with spatial resolution. For high-resolution CT, full global self-attention can exceed practical GPU memory budgets. Recent 3D transformer variants address this with windowed or sparse attention, hierarchical processing, and early spatial downsampling to keep training and inference feasible.

\section{Vision--Language Models}\label{sec:rw_vlm}
%% ========================================================

Vision--language models (VLMs) aim to connect visual perception and natural language understanding. These models learn joint representations of images and text, enabling cross-modal tasks such as image captioning, visual question answering (VQA), and multimodal reasoning.

\subsection{Contrastive Alignment and the CLIP Paradigm}

CLIP (Contrastive Language--Image Pre-training)~\cite{radford2021learning} introduced a scalable approach to learning visual representations from natural language supervision. By training independent vision and text encoders to maximize the cosine similarity of matching image--text pairs in a shared embedding space while minimizing the similarity of non-matching pairs, CLIP learns transferable visual concepts. Trained on 400 million image--text pairs curated from the internet, CLIP showed strong zero-shot transfer capability across many downstream visual tasks.

Recent advances refined the CLIP objective. SigLIP (Sigmoid Loss for Language Image Pre-Training)~\cite{zhai2023siglip} replaces the standard softmax-based contrastive loss with a pairwise sigmoid loss. This change removes the need for global views of all negative pairs across devices, improves training efficiency and scalability, and can improve representation quality. The contrastive learning paradigm established by CLIP and extended by SigLIP remains a common foundation for aligning vision and language modalities in modern VLMs.

\subsection{Generative VLMs and Open-Weight Ecosystems}

While contrastive models focus on alignment and retrieval, generative VLMs produce natural language outputs explicitly conditioned on visual inputs. BLIP (Bootstrapping Language-Image Pre-training)~\cite{li2022blip} proposed a unified framework that jointly trains a contrastive alignment objective, an image--text matching classification objective, and a generative language modeling objective. This multi-task design made BLIP effective for both image understanding and text generation.

Building upon this, BLIP-2~\cite{li2023blip2} introduced a compute-efficient strategy for bridging the modality gap: the Q-Former (Query Transformer). The Q-Former acts as an interface between a frozen image encoder and a frozen large language model. It uses learnable query vectors that cross-attend to dense visual features and extract text-relevant information. In this way, the Q-Former compresses thousands of spatial visual tokens into a fixed, small number of latent tokens; a common setting uses 32 tokens. These compressed tokens are then projected into the text embedding space of the frozen LLM. This architecture showed that strong multimodal behavior is possible without fully unfreezing very large language and vision backbones.

A related strategy for high-density visual signals was adopted by DeepMind's Flamingo~\cite{alayrac2022flamingo}. Rather than a Q-Former, Flamingo used a Perceiver Resampler mechanism, which also cross-attends to large input arrays such as high-resolution images or variable-length videos, using a small set of latent learnable query vectors. Flamingo then interleaved these compressed visual tokens with standard text tokens inside a frozen LLM through gated cross-attention layers and reported strong few-shot and in-context reasoning behavior.

LLaVA (Large Language-and-Vision Assistant)~\cite{liu2024visual} simplified these bridging architectures by showing that a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) projection layer can map visual features from a pretrained CLIP encoder into the vocabulary embedding space of an LLM, without using Q-Formers or Resamplers. Through visual instruction tuning on image-text instruction data, LLaVA reported strong multimodal dialogue performance. More recently, open-source initiatives expanded VLM development. Models like InternVL~\cite{chen2023internvl} focus on scaling the vision foundation model for broad visual-linguistic tasks, while the Molmo family~\cite{deitke2024molmo} emphasizes curated open multimodal data and efficient projector architectures.

\subsection{Medical Vision-Language Models}

Adapting VLMs to the medical domain presents additional challenges. Medical images, including high-resolution multi-parametric MRIs and chest X-rays, have different characteristics from natural images in datasets such as COCO and ImageNet. Furthermore, clinical texts use specialized vocabulary, and tolerance for factual error in medical AI is low.

Early attempts to bridge this gap focused on domain-specific contrastive pretraining. BiomedCLIP~\cite{zhang2023biomedclip} pretrained a CLIP-style architecture from scratch on 15 million biomedical image--text pairs sourced from PubMed Central articles, showing that in-domain pretraining improves downstream biomedical classification and retrieval compared to generic CLIP models.

For 2D clinical radiology, development has accelerated in recent years~\cite{wang2025rrgsurvey}. CheXagent~\cite{chen2024chexagent} was introduced as a clinical foundation model for chest X-ray interpretation, fine-tuned on large corpora of radiology reports to interpret images and answer clinical questions. MAIRA-2~\cite{bannur2024maira} advanced grounded report generation by linking generated assertions to localized bounding boxes in the original X-ray, reducing unsupported findings and improving trustworthiness.

For 3D medical imaging, the challenge is more severe because volumetric data is large and paired text data is limited. Hamamci et al.~\cite{hamamci2024foundation} introduced CT-CLIP, a contrastive learning framework engineered for 3D computed tomography. Trained on the CT-RATE dataset, which pairs 3D volumetric CT scans with corresponding free-text radiology reports, CT-CLIP learns 3D structural visual representations aligned with clinical text. This work is among the earliest large-scale efforts to apply vision--language pretraining directly to volumetric medical data.

MedGemma~\cite{yang2024medgemma} is a family of medical foundation models built on top of the Gemma architecture~\cite{team2024gemma}. It inherits the general-language capabilities of Gemma and adds continued medical pretraining and instruction tuning on biomedical literature, medical exams, and de-identified clinical notes. The instruction-tuned variant MedGemma-4B-IT reports strong zero-shot and few-shot performance on medical reasoning tasks. In this thesis, MedGemma serves as one of the core decoder families evaluated in the report-generation pipeline.

\section{Decoders and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning}\label{sec:rw_llm}
%% ========================================================

Generating clinically useful radiology reports from visual features requires a strong language decoder. Modern language generation models mainly follow two architectural paradigms: Encoder-Decoder models and Causal Language Models. Understanding the differences between these paradigms is important when selecting a backbone for medical vision-language systems.

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Architectures vs. Causal Modeling}

Encoder-Decoder models, such as BART (Bidirectional and Auto-Regressive Transformers)~\cite{lewis2019bart} and T5, use a bidirectional encoder to process input context and an autoregressive decoder to generate output tokens. Because the encoder attends to both left and right context during prompt processing, these models work well for tasks that require full-context understanding before generation, such as summarization and machine translation. BART is pretrained by corrupting documents with token masking, deletion, and sentence permutation, then optimizing a reconstruction loss.

In the biomedical domain, BioBART~\cite{yuan2022biobart} adapts this architecture by continually pretraining the baseline BART model on a large corpus of PubMed abstracts. This domain-specific pretraining helps BioBART retain BART's strong generation quality while adapting vocabulary and latent representations to biomedical terminology. In standard vision-language pipelines using Encoder-Decoder backbones, visual embeddings are typically fed directly into the bidirectional encoder, so the model can form a global contextual representation before autoregressive decoding begins.

Conversely, Causal Language Models (CLMs) use a decoder-only architecture that predicts the next token using only preceding tokens. GPT-2 (Generative Pre-trained Transformer 2)~\cite{radford2019language} popularized this scalable paradigm and showed that unsupervised next-token prediction on large web corpora can produce coherent zero-shot text generation. The base GPT-2 model has 124M parameters, and its clean architecture makes it a practical baseline for diagnostic report-generation experiments under limited compute.

Unlike bidirectional encoders, causal models process the conditioning prompt autoregressively, including prepended visual tokens used as a soft prompt. This means left and right context are not mixed in a single bidirectional encoding step, but large modern causal models can still capture rich dependencies through scale and training data.

Recent foundation models have scaled the decoder-only paradigm to tens of billions of parameters. The LLaMA family~\cite{touvron2023llama} and the Gemma family~\cite{team2024gemma} have shown strong performance in natural language understanding, reasoning, and instruction following. MedGemma~\cite{yang2024medgemma}, including the instruction-tuned MedGemma-4B-IT variant, extends these capabilities with continued medical pretraining and instruction tuning. In causal VLM architectures, preserving the spatial and semantic quality of the visual token prefix is important because downstream generation depends on this initial visual context.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)}

Adapting massive decoders, especially multi-billion parameter foundation models such as MedGemma, to specialized tasks like organ-specific radiology report generation through full fine-tuning is often computationally prohibitive and can degrade general language behavior. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods address this by updating only a small fraction of model parameters while keeping most pretrained weights frozen.

Early PEFT strategies included Adapter modules~\cite{houlsby2019parameter}, which introduced small trainable feed-forward bottleneck networks between frozen multi-head attention and feed-forward transformer sub-layers. Another approach, Prefix-Tuning~\cite{li2021prefix}, optimizes a sequence of continuous task-specific prompt vectors prepended to transformer layers. While effective in reducing forgetting, these methods alter the network topology and can introduce additional inference latency or reduce available context length.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)~\cite{hu2022lora} addresses these issues and is now widely adopted in foundation-model fine-tuning. LoRA assumes that required updates to dense weight matrices have low intrinsic rank. It introduces trainable low-rank matrices in parallel with frozen linear layers, most often query and value projections in self-attention. Instead of updating the full matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$, LoRA learns smaller matrices $A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ where $r \ll \min(d, k)$, yielding $W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + B A$. Because $B A$ can be merged into $W_0$ for deployment, LoRA does not add extra inference latency after merging.

To further reduce training cost, QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} combines LoRA with 4-bit Normal Float (NF4) double quantization. In QLoRA, frozen pretrained model weights are stored in 4-bit precision, while only the lightweight trainable LoRA adapter matrices are cast to higher precision such as BFloat16 during backpropagation. This combination substantially reduces VRAM requirements for fine-tuning multi-billion parameter foundation models and enables faster iteration on standard hardware.

\section{Radiology Report Generation}\label{sec:rw_reportgen}
%% ========================================================

Automatic radiology report generation aims to produce clinically accurate free-text reports from medical images. This task is more demanding than generic image captioning because generated reports must remain factually correct, cover relevant pathological findings, explicitly state negative findings when needed, and follow clinical reporting conventions.

\subsection{Traditional and Early Transformer Approaches}

Early approaches to radiology report generation adapted encoder--decoder architectures from natural image captioning. Jing et al.~\cite{jing2018automatic} proposed a co-attention mechanism combined with a hierarchical LSTM decoder to generate structured medical reports from chest X-rays. However, these CNN--RNN frameworks often produced generic and repetitive descriptions, and they had difficulty capturing rare or subtle findings under strong data imbalance.

Transformer-based methods later improved report quality by tracking long-range context more effectively. Chen et al.~\cite{chen2020generating} introduced a memory-driven transformer with a relational memory module to retain key clinical information during autoregressive generation, which improved coherence in longer reports. In follow-up work, Chen et al.~\cite{chen2022crossmodal} proposed cross-modal memory networks with separate but interacting memory banks for visual and textual features, enabling stronger cross-modal reasoning. Nicolson et al.~\cite{nicolson2023improving} further showed that warm-starting both the vision encoder from sources such as ImageNet or the CheXpert dataset~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} and the language decoder from models such as clinical BERT can substantially improve chest X-ray report generation compared with training from scratch.

\subsection{The Evaluation Paradigm: From N-Grams to Clinical Efficacy}

Early report-generation studies relied heavily on lexical overlap metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR. In medical reporting, lexical overlap alone is insufficient because clinically meaningful errors can occur with small wording changes. The field therefore shifted toward clinically grounded metrics such as CheXbert~\cite{smit2020chexbert}, RadGraph~\cite{jain2021radgraph}, and GREEN~\cite{ostmeier2024green}, which better reflect factual and relational correctness. In this thesis, the high-level motivation is introduced here, while the operational metric interpretation policy is defined in Section~\ref{sec:metric_interpretation_policy}.

\subsection{2D Radiology Foundation Models}

As the field moved toward foundation models, researchers began adapting large frozen language models as report decoders. R2GenGPT~\cite{wang2023r2gengpt} showed that combining a frozen LLM with a trainable visual encoder and a lightweight projection layer can generate radiology reports and achieve competitive results on common natural language generation and clinical efficacy metrics. This setup benefits from the language knowledge in pretrained LLMs while keeping multimodal training costs manageable.

Recently, foundation models tailored for 2D X-ray interpretation have emerged. CheXagent~\cite{chen2024chexagent} is a clinical foundation model for chest X-rays. By fine-tuning a large VLM on radiology reports and visual question-answering pairs, CheXagent reported strong performance on both report generation and interactive clinical question-answering tasks. 

At the same time, the architectural focus shifted from linguistic fluency toward clinical grounding. MAIRA-2~\cite{bannur2024maira} introduced grounded radiology report generation with localized grounding of reported findings in the original X-ray. This grounding reduced unsupported findings and addressed an important safety requirement for medical deployment. 

\subsection{3D Volumetric Report Generation}

While 2D X-ray interpretation has matured rapidly, 3D volumetric report generation from CT and MRI scans remains an open and challenging frontier. The shift from 2D to 3D sharply increases computational complexity in visual token processing. In addition, before the release of CT-RATE~\cite{hamamci2024foundation}, paired 3D volumes and free-text reports were scarce.

The release of CT-RATE spurred the rapid development of several pioneering 3D multimodal foundation models:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CT2Rep}~\cite{hamamci2024ct2rep}: As one of the earliest works using CT-RATE, CT2Rep showed that automated report generation for 3D medical imaging is feasible when 3D visual tokens are projected into a suitable language decoder.
    \item \textbf{M3D}~\cite{bai2024m3d}: M3D proposed strategies to compress volumetric data into spatial tokens more efficiently, enabling LLM-based processing of full CT studies under context-length constraints.
    \item \textbf{Argus}~\cite{argus2024}: Argus presented benchmarking and model-improvement strategies for 3D radiology VLMs, from 3B-scale models to 70B-scale models, and reported that larger language models tend to improve clinical efficacy metrics such as GREEN.
    \item \textbf{Med3DVLM}~\cite{med3dvlm2024} and \textbf{RadFM}~\cite{radfm2024}: Both models introduced unified 3D vision-language frameworks that emphasize cross-modal alignment between volumetric voxels and clinical text.
\end{itemize}

Recent work also emphasized fine-grained anatomical supervision for CT understanding. fVLM~\cite{shui2025fvlm} introduced large-scale and fine-grained vision-language pretraining for CT and reported strong gains on report-generation benchmarks. Its organ-aware supervision and decomposition perspective directly motivates the organ-guided decomposition strategy used in this thesis.

A key challenge across these works is token compression. A raw 3D CT volume contains far more information than a 2D image, and naive patch extraction can produce tens of thousands of tokens that overwhelm the LLM decoder. Recent evidence~\cite{hamamci2025bettertokensbetter3d} shows that tokenization design is a primary performance driver in 3D VLMs: the paper highlights limitations of slice-wise tokenization and reports gains from compact volumetric tokens trained with a curriculum that includes local reconstruction, overlapping-window tiling, and long-context refinement.

\section{3D Medical Image Segmentation and Organ-Guided Attention}\label{sec:rw_3dseg}
%% ========================================================

Accurate anatomical localization is essential for generating specific and clinically actionable radiology reports. The availability of automated whole-body 3D segmentation tools has enabled VLM architectures that use anatomical priors to ground text generation.

\subsection{Volumetric Segmentation Frameworks}

While early segmentation models relied on 3D CNNs such as 3D U-Net, transformer-based architectures have become prominent by capturing long-range contextual dependencies more effectively. TransUNet~\cite{chen2021transunet} and Swin-UNet~\cite{cao2023swin} demonstrated strong results for 2D medical segmentation, but volumetric data requires dedicated 3D processing. 

nnFormer (Interleaved Transformer for Volumetric Medical Image Segmentation)~\cite{zhou2021nnformer} extends transformer capabilities to 3D medical images. By interleaving local 3D convolution operations with global multi-head self-attention, nnFormer captures both local texture cues and broader geometric relationships between organs, and reports strong benchmarks on multiple 3D datasets.

In practical clinical pipelines, TotalSegmentator~\cite{wasserthal2023totalsegmentator} is a widely used tool for automatic segmentation. Built on the nnU-Net framework, it provides out-of-the-box segmentation of 104 anatomical structures, including major organs, bones, vessels, and muscles, from standard CT images. The resulting 3D masks can serve as precise anatomical priors for downstream generative tasks.

\subsection{Organ-Guided Attention and Clinical Grounding}

Incorporating these anatomical priors into VLM architectures enables a shift from global image captioning to localized organ-specific reasoning. Rather than processing an entire CT volume as a uniform sequence of visual tokens, organ-guided mechanisms restrict or bias cross-attention to regions associated with specific query structures such as the lungs or liver.

This approach draws on learnable query architectures such as the Perceiver~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver}, where a fixed set of latent vectors attends to a large sensory input. In medical settings, these latent vectors can represent specific organs. By masking visual features with 3D segmentation maps before cross-attention, the language decoder is constrained to focus on relevant anatomy when generating each report segment; recent organ-mask-guided report generation studies support this design direction~\cite{gu2024comg,li2024organaware}.

Recent radiology report generation work also shows that auxiliary clinical objectives can strengthen organ-level representations during training~\cite{su2024tgmt,li2024organaware}. Joint optimization with diagnosis-oriented signals such as disease prediction, terminology prediction, or organ-aware diagnosis losses encourages the visual encoder to separate subtle abnormal cues in anatomically relevant regions. This additional supervision provides a practical inductive bias for more clinically grounded report generation.
